Abdul Kalam, widely known as the "Missile Man of India," was a renowned aerospace scientist and the 11th President of India. Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, he played a pivotal role in advancing India's missile and space programs. His leadership in projects like the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP) earned him this moniker. Kalam's contributions were not limited to scientific achievements; he was also a visionary educator and inspirational leader. His presidency from 2002 to 2007 was marked by his efforts to bridge the gap between science and society, promoting technology as a means for national development. His legacy endures in his profound impact on India's scientific community and his unwavering commitment to inspiring the youth.

Biography

  • Early Life and Education: Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Kalam came from a humble background and pursued physics and aerospace engineering at Madras Institute of Technology.
  • ISRO Contribution: Kalam joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1969, where he was a part of the team that developed India's first satellite launch vehicle, SLV-III, which successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in 1980.
  • Presidency: Serving as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007, Kalam was known for his accessibility, earning the nickname "People's President" for his efforts to connect with the citizenry, particularly the youth.
  • Pokhran-II Nuclear Tests: Kalam played a crucial role as the Chief Scientific Advisor to the Prime Minister during India's nuclear tests in 1998, bolstering India's position as a nuclear state.
